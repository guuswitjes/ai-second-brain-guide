The Concept

Your AI Should Think Like You

Most people use AI like a search engine with better grammar. They open ChatGPT, type a question, get an answer, close the tab. Every conversation starts from zero.

An AI Second Brain is different. It knows your voice, your expertise, your decision-making patterns. It knows what words you never use. It knows the difference between your professional tone and your personal one. It does not need you to explain who you are every single time.

This guide shows you how to build one. Not theory. A working system using Claude Code that you can set up this week.

The Problem

Why a Second Brain, Not Just a Better Prompt

A good prompt gets you a good answer. A Second Brain gets you a good answer that sounds like you, applies your judgment, follows your rules, and fits the context you are working in. Every time. Without repeating yourself.

Without a Second Brain

  • Every conversation starts from zero
  • You re-explain your style every time
  • Output sounds generic
  • No memory between sessions
  • Same mistakes repeated
  • Work stays in silos

With a Second Brain

  • AI knows your context from the start
  • Voice and rules are built in
  • Output sounds like you
  • Knowledge compounds over time
  • Guardrails prevent recurring errors
  • Skills chain into workflows
The difference between using AI and having an AI team is this: one answers questions, the other anticipates them.

🗺️ CLAUDE.md — The Roadmap

Claude Code reads this file automatically every time a conversation starts. It is the single file that shapes everything the AI does for you.

Think of it as the onboarding document you give a new team member. Not the full manual. Just enough to know what the company does, where things are, and what the house rules are.

How to build it

1

Create a file called CLAUDE.md in your project root. Write 3-5 lines about who you are and what this workspace is for.

2

Add a "Your Role" section that tells the AI what perspective to take. "You are my marketing assistant" is better than nothing.

3

Add references to your other files. Write "See: _context/voice-style.md" instead of copying content in. Point, never copy.

4

Add guardrails: what the AI must always do, and must never do. "Never publish without approval. Company data stays in this workspace."

Example

# CLAUDE.md — My Workspace
## Who I Am
Marketing lead at [company]. Content, campaigns, team of 4.
## Your Role
You are my AI marketing assistant. Direct, data-driven, UK English.
See: _context/voice-style.md for tone
See: _context/writing-rules.md for grammar
## Guardrails
- Never publish content without my approval
- Company data stays in this workspace

Key rule: CLAUDE.md is a roadmap, not a manual. It points to files, never copies their content. One source of truth per concept. Update in one place, used everywhere.

🧬 _context/ — The Identity Layer

This is what makes AI output sound like you instead of generic. Without it, the AI writes like "a helpful assistant." With it, the AI writes like someone who has worked with you for months.

Four files capture your professional identity. Every skill and agent you build later inherits them automatically.

KNOWLEDGE
What I know
3-5 expertise areas with frameworks
JUDGMENT
How I decide
4-6 decision principles
VOICE
How I sound
Tone, metaphors, structure
RULES
How I write
Grammar, format, banlist

How to build it

1

Start with voice-style.md

3-4 adjectives for your tone. Your typical structure (e.g. "Problem, then solution, then action"). What you never sound like. Easiest file to test.

2

Add writing-rules.md

Sentence length, punctuation, and a banlist: 20+ words the AI loves but you would never use. "Delve", "streamline", "cutting-edge". Banning them forces the AI to find alternatives that sound like you.

3

Add knowledge-domains.md and judgment-patterns.md

Add these as you notice the AI making decisions you disagree with. They teach the AI your expertise and your reasoning patterns.

Tip: Each file should be 50-150 lines. Longer than that and you are overwriting. The AI needs direction, not a novel.

⚡ .claude/ — The Automation Layer

Without this, you type the same instructions every time. With it, you say "write a post" and the AI runs the full workflow: loads your voice, applies your content strategy, uses your hook templates.

Two building blocks: agents (routers that decide what to do) and skills (specialists that do the actual work).

🤖

Agent

The orchestrator. It does not do the work itself. It routes your request to the right skill or chains multiple skills together.

"Handle content" could mean write, repurpose, schedule, or validate. The agent decides.

⚡

Skill

The executor. Does one thing well. Has a SKILL.md with instructions and a knowledge/ folder with reference files. Can work standalone or as part of an agent.

"Write a LinkedIn post in my voice, following my content strategy."

How to build it

1

Pick one task you do weekly

Something repeatable with a clear output. Writing posts, generating reports, auditing pages.

2

Create a skill folder with SKILL.md

Write trigger words (when to activate), workflow steps (what to do), and output format (what the result looks like).

3

Add a knowledge/ subfolder

Templates, examples, frameworks. The reference material the skill needs to do its job well.

4

Test, adjust, expand

Test with real work. When you have 3+ related skills, create an agent to route between them.

Example: skill anatomy

.claude/skills/linkedin-post-generator/
SKILL.md — trigger words + workflow steps
knowledge/
post-frameworks.md — content structures
hook-templates.md — 12 opening line patterns
performance-insights.md — what works (from data)

The rule: Single task = skill. Multiple related tasks = agent. Start with skills. Agents emerge naturally when you have enough skills that grouping them makes sense.

The Core

The Identity Layer: Teaching AI Who You Are

This is the part most people skip, and it is the part that makes the biggest difference. Four files in _context/ that together define your professional identity. Once written, every skill and agent you build inherits them automatically.

1

KNOWLEDGE DOMAINS

What you know. Your areas of expertise.

▶

List 3-5 domains where you have real expertise. Not everything you know. The areas where you can make judgment calls that a generic AI cannot.

For each domain, describe: what you know, how deep your knowledge goes, what frameworks or mental models you use, and what makes your perspective different from the mainstream.

Example

## AI Adoption & Practical Application
I frame AI as a colleague, not a tool. My background in [field] translates directly to helping teams adopt AI because [specific reason]. I use the [framework name] approach: [brief description].

2

JUDGMENT PATTERNS

How you decide. Your decision-making principles.

▶

These are the rules that guide your decisions when there is no clear right answer. The patterns that people who work with you would recognise as "that is so you."

Write 4-6 judgment patterns. Each one should describe: the principle, when it applies, and a concrete example of how it plays out in practice.

Example

## Start and Adjust
Launch when valuable, not when perfect. Living documents beat polished ones that never ship. I gather feedback from practice, not from planning meetings. If 80% is good enough to learn from, ship it.

3

VOICE STYLE

How you sound. Your tone, metaphors, patterns.

▶

This is what makes AI output sound like you instead of generic. Include: your core tone (3-4 adjectives), your typical sentence structure, metaphors you naturally use, phrases you say often, and what you never sound like.

The "what you never sound like" part is just as important. If you are direct, say "never hedging or diplomatic." If you use humour, describe what kind.

Example

Core tone: Direct, practical, honest.
Structure: Problem → Solution → Action. Always.
Metaphors: AI as colleague, not tool. Newsletter as architecture, not content.
Never: corporate speak, hedging, passive voice, "I would suggest..."

4

WRITING RULES

How you write. Grammar, format, constraints.

▶

The mechanical rules. Sentence length, punctuation preferences, formatting standards, and a list of words you never use (a "banlist").

The banlist is surprisingly powerful. AI loves words like "delve", "leverage", "streamline", "cutting-edge". If those words are not in your vocabulary, ban them. The AI will find better alternatives that sound like you.

Example

Sentences: Short. Active voice. Fragments OK for rhythm.
Punctuation: No exclamation marks. No em dashes.
Language: UK English, not US.
Banned: delve, leverage, streamline, cutting-edge, game-changer, deep dive, at the end of the day...

How to write your pillar files

Do not try to write these in one sitting. Start with voice-style.md (easiest to test) and writing-rules.md (easiest to define). Then add knowledge-domains and judgment-patterns as you notice the AI making decisions you disagree with.

Each file should be 50-150 lines. Longer than that and you are overwriting. The AI needs direction, not a novel.

The Automation Layer

Agents and Skills: Building Your AI Team

Once your identity layer is in place, you can build reusable automations. There are two types: agents (they decide what to do) and skills (they do the actual work). An agent orchestrates. A skill executes. Skills can work standalone or as part of an agent.

🤖

AGENT

An orchestrator. It does not do the work itself. It routes your request to the right skill or chain of skills.

Has an AGENT.md (~100 lines)
Contains a routing table (trigger → skill)
Defines chains (skill A → skill B → skill C)
Has guardrails (what it must never do)
Examples:
content-creator (18 skills), seo-optimizer (8 skills), today (5 skills)
⚡

SKILL

A single-task executor. Does one thing well. Can work standalone or as part of an agent. Think of it as a specialist on your team.

Has a SKILL.md with instructions
Has a knowledge/ folder with reference files
Reads _context/ pillar files for voice and rules
Triggered by specific words or phrases
Examples:
linkedin-post-generator, meal-planner, seo-page-auditor, session-report

Skill Anatomy

.claude/skills/linkedin-post-generator/
SKILL.md — trigger words, workflow steps, output format
knowledge/
post-frameworks.md — content structures
hook-templates.md — opening line patterns
performance-insights.md — what works based on data

Inside a SKILL.md

Every skill starts with YAML frontmatter. This tells the AI when to activate it and what it does. Below that: the actual workflow instructions.

---
name: linkedin-post-generator
description: Generates a LinkedIn post based on brand voice and content frameworks.
patterns:
- "write a linkedin post"
- "generate social media content"
---
# Workflow Steps
## 1. Load context files (_context/voice-style.md, writing-rules.md)
## 2. Determine audience persona
## 3. Generate post with hook, body, takeaway, CTA
## 4. Run quality check against banlist

The patterns field is the magic: when you type "write a linkedin post", the AI automatically loads this skill and follows the workflow. No manual file pointing needed.

The Decision Rule

When should something be an agent vs. a skill?

Make it an AGENT when:
It needs to route. "Handle content" could mean write, repurpose, schedule, or validate. That is an agent with skills underneath.
Make it a SKILL when:
It does one specific thing. "Write a LinkedIn post." "Audit an SEO page." "Generate a meal plan." Skills are the building blocks.
Critical Concept

Context Isolation: Keeping Worlds Separate

If you use AI for more than one role (and most people do), you need context isolation. It means: data from one context never leaks into another. Your employer's information never shows up in your side project content. Your personal health data never appears in work output.

How It Works

Each world has its own CLAUDE.md, its own _context/ with pillar files, and its own .claude/ with skills and agents. They inherit shared patterns from the root (your core identity stays consistent), but they add their own voice, rules and knowledge on top.

The CLAUDE.md in each world states explicitly: "Data from this context never appears in [other context]." The AI follows this instruction because it is in the file it reads on every start.

Example: The Daily Briefing

Imagine saying "good morning" and getting back:

⚡Energy: Deep sleep 1h42m (good). Best focus window: 9-12. Schedule meetings after 14:00.
📅Agenda: 3 meetings today. 10:00 team sync, 13:00 client call, 15:30 1-on-1. Gap at 11:00 for deep work.

This is built with 5 skills chained through one agent. Each skill calls its MCP server, formats the data, and the agent combines it into one briefing. All running locally on your machine.

Securing Your API Keys

MCP servers need API keys to connect. Never store those keys inside your workspace. The AI can read your workspace files, which means it can see any key you put there. Instead: store keys outside the workspace and inject them as environment variables at startup.

DO NOT
// .claude/mcp/mcp.json
"api_key": "sk-abc123..."
Key sits in workspace. AI can read it. Git can leak it.
DO
// secrets folder (outside workspace)
~/.secrets/clickup-api-key.txt
// startup script loads them
export CLICKUP_KEY=$(cat ~/.secrets/...)
Key stays outside. Injected at runtime. Never in git.

Also add your secrets folder, .env files, and mcp.json to .gitignore. If you ever accidentally commit a key: rotate it immediately, do not just delete the commit.

Action

Getting Started This Week

You do not need to build everything at once. Start small, iterate, expand. Here is a practical sequence.

1

CREATE YOUR WORKSPACE

Create a folder. Add CLAUDE.md. Write one paragraph about who you are and what this workspace is for. That is enough to start.

2

WRITE YOUR VOICE STYLE

Create _context/voice-style.md. Describe how you sound in 3-4 adjectives, your typical structure, and 10 words you never use. Test it by asking the AI to write something and see if it sounds like you.

3

ADD WRITING RULES

Create _context/writing-rules.md. Sentence length, punctuation preferences, and a banlist of AI words you hate. Start with 20 banned words and grow it over time.

4

BUILD YOUR FIRST SKILL

Pick one task you do at least weekly. Create .claude/skills/[task-name]/SKILL.md with clear instructions. Add a knowledge/ folder with reference material. Test it with real work.

5

ITERATE AND EXPAND

Use it for a week. Notice what the AI gets wrong. Update your pillar files. Add more skills. When you have 3+ related skills, create an agent to route between them. Grow organically.

The Rule That Matters Most

Do not build more than you can test. Build one skill, use it for a week, iterate. Then build the next one. A Second Brain grows through use, not through architecture. Three skills you use daily are worth more than thirty you built but never touch.

START SMALL. ITERATE. COMPOUND.
